This work addresses probability of default on LendingClub-style consumer loans: from application-time information, estimate the chance a loan will end in default (here, charged off) rather than being fully repaid. The task is inherently imbalanced and noisy, because many borrowers look similar on paper yet a minority default, and the label reflects outcomes years after origination.

The modeling stack spans three families on purpose. Logistic regression gives a transparent, strongly regularized linear boundary in a transformed feature space, which is easy to audit and stable for baseline comparisons. Random forest averages many depth-limited trees with bagging, capturing nonlinearities and interactions without hand-engineering them while remaining relatively robust. XGBoost gradient-boosted trees push flexibility further, sequentially fitting shallow trees to correct residuals, which often yields the best ranking performance on heterogeneous tabular credit data when leakage has been removed. Together they bracket simple versus flexible learners so gains (or failures) from complexity are visible rather than assumed.

On the held-out test split, XGBoost achieved the highest ROC AUC at about 0.7383, with random forest at about 0.7357 and logistic regression at about 0.7345, so all three rank similarly but XGBoost edges ahead on discrimination. The same ordering appears for the Brier score, where lower is better: XGBoost about 0.1406, random forest about 0.1413, and logistic regression about 0.2047, so XGBoost remains best overall while random forest improved probability quality substantially after tuning. ROC AUC and Brier need not agree in general, because AUC only cares about how well predicted probabilities order positives above negatives, while Brier is a proper score that penalizes both wrong ordering and poorly calibrated magnitudes (overconfident wrong predictions hurt Brier more than they need to hurt AUC). A model could rank well yet be miscalibrated and lose on Brier, or be conservative and calibrated yet miss some rankable separation; here the leader on ranking also produced the sharpest, best-calibrated probabilities among the three.

SHAP importance on XGBoost highlights five drivers that align with how underwriters think about risk. The strongest signal is the loan term encoded as thirty-six versus other terms: shorter contracts concentrate payment obligations differently than longer ones and correlate with product and borrower segments, so they naturally move default risk. Accounts opened in the last twenty-four months reflects recent credit-seeking behavior, which often precedes stress or stacking of new obligations. Debt-to-income ratio measures how much of stated income is already committed to debt service, directly squeezing capacity to absorb shocks. Installment-to-income is an engineered burden measure tying the monthly payment to earnings, which isolates whether the new loan payment is affordable even when DTI is defined differently across files. Finally, the low end of the FICO range band is a coarse but standard creditworthiness summary: lower values indicate weaker historical repayment performance and higher odds of future distress.

For bias and variance on this dataset, logistic regression behaves like a high-bias, lower-variance model: it cannot fit every kink in the data, so it leaves signal on the table (worse Brier and more miscalibration as seen in higher ECE in the metrics file) but its decision surface is stable across samples. XGBoost sits at the low-bias, higher-variance end: it can fit subtle patterns and interactions, which lifts AUC and Brier here, but it can also chase noise if leakage or small-sample artifacts were present; the modest gap between models after the leakage fix suggests the signal left in the features is real but not enormous, which is what we expect in retail credit.

An earlier pipeline that kept post-origination payment and recovery fields (for example totals paid, recoveries, last payment dates, and similar columns) effectively let the model read the future: those fields are determined after the loan is underway and collapse toward the eventual outcome, inflating AUC toward unrealistically high values near 0.99. Removing them forces the model to rely only on information plausibly known at approval, which drops AUC into the low seventies on this sample—uncomfortable for a slide deck but credible for out-of-time consumer lending where overlap between good and bad borrowers is large. The current results are therefore more believable for decision-making and regulation because they resemble industry experience rather than a leakage artifact.

At a probability threshold of 0.40 on the test set, XGBoost’s confusion matrix implies the following for a lender treating “approve” as predicted probability at or above the threshold. Nine thousand eight hundred fifty-two loans that would have been fully paid were correctly approved (true negatives), while six hundred ninety-two fully paid loans were wrongly declined (false positives), sacrificing revenue from good customers to avoid risk. One thousand nine hundred sixty-five defaulted loans were mistakenly approved (false negatives), exposing the book to losses that a perfect score would have caught, while six hundred seventy-five defaults were correctly declined (true positives). In business terms, the bank trades off rejecting some good borrowers against catching a minority of bad ones; tightening the threshold would reduce false negatives among defaulters but increase false positives among good borrowers, and the right balance depends on margin, capital, and risk appetite rather than accuracy alone.

Ablation results reinforce this model-family interpretation: when the engineered feature bundle was removed for the tree-based pipeline, test ROC AUC moved from 0.7365 to 0.7372, indicating no practical discrimination gain from those manual transforms in boosted trees. This is consistent with how XGBoost works, because tree splits already learn nonlinear thresholds and interaction structure that many hand-crafted monotonic transforms are trying to approximate. The same engineered variables can still be valuable for higher-bias linear models such as logistic regression, where explicit nonlinear transforms and ratio features often supply curvature the model cannot create on its own.

The roughly twenty-six percent default recall at the 0.40 operating threshold (about 25.80% in the current test run) should be read as a deliberate credit-policy choice rather than a modeling error: this higher threshold is conservative about flagging bad loans and therefore avoids falsely rejecting too many good borrowers. Validation-based threshold optimization for the tuned XGBoost model selected 0.21, where recall rises to about 62.9%, making the operating point materially more aggressive in catching future charge-offs while increasing the chance of rejecting borrowers who would have repaid. In practice this is a business frontier between growth and protection, and leadership should set the cutoff by portfolio loss tolerance, unit economics, and customer-acquisition goals instead of maximizing a single metric in isolation.

In the extended leave-one-group-out ablation, removing loan-term features had by far the largest downside (AUC delta -0.0142), while removing DTI or FICO groups produced only small declines (about -0.00095 and -0.00082), and removing income features slightly improved AUC (+0.0010). This pattern indicates that term structure is the strongest standalone driver in the current processed feature space, whereas income transforms are largely redundant for boosted trees once the rest of the tabular interactions are available.

The final model comparison is also consistent with standard ML bias-variance theory on tabular credit risk. Logistic regression shows high bias and low variance behavior: even after convergence improvements it reaches ROC AUC 0.7345 but has the weakest probability quality (Brier 0.2047), which is what we expect when a mostly linear hypothesis class underfits nonlinear borrower-risk structure. Random forest reduces bias through bagging and nonlinear partitions with moderate variance, and after tuning it improves to ROC AUC 0.7357 with Brier 0.1413 (from roughly 0.7333 and 0.1629 before tuning), while XGBoost keeps the lowest effective bias with boosting and regularized depth/learning-rate control, improving from ROC AUC 0.7376 to 0.7383 and maintaining the best combined discrimination and calibration (Brier 0.1406).

SMOTE comparison on validation indicates that oversampling hurt all three models (LR delta -0.000449, RF delta -0.006926, XGBoost delta -0.003340), which is consistent with a setting where class representation after target filtering is already sufficient for tree and linear learners to separate signal without synthetic minority examples.
